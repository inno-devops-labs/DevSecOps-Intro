### Why the difference?
- GHSA-23c5-xmqv-rm74 was found by Grype and was not present in Trivy's unique CVE set. Grype mapped it to `minimatch` `3.0.5` with severity `High` and fixed versions `3.1.4`. The likely reason is database cadence or package-matching/fix-version logic: in this run, the CVE appears in the SBOM-based Grype result but not in Trivy's direct image result.
- CVE-2015-9235 was found by Trivy and was not present in Grype's unique CVE set. Trivy mapped it to `jsonwebtoken` `0.1.0` in target `Node.js` with severity `CRITICAL` and fixed version `4.2.2`. The likely reason is database cadence or package-matching/fix-version logic: Trivy scanned the image directly with its own vulnerability DB, while Grype scanned the CycloneDX SBOM generated by Syft.


### When would you pick each?
I would pick Syft+Grype when I need a durable inventory artifact, not only a one-time scan. The decoupled model lets me generate one SBOM, commit or attest it, and then rescan the same inventory later as vulnerability databases change. That makes it the better fit for SBOM-as-attestation workflows and for Lab 8, where the SBOM becomes the predicate being signed.

I would pick Trivy when I want the simplest CI step with broad coverage from one tool. It can scan the image directly and also supports related checks such as filesystem, IaC, secrets, and misconfiguration scanning, so it is easier to drop into a pipeline when the goal is fast developer feedback rather than preserving a separately signed SBOM.
